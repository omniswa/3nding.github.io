QR Code Phishing Attacks Up 146%: What Microsoft's Q1 Data Reveals

May 2, 2026 8-Minute Read

The email arrived looking legitimate: a PDF attachment, a familiar corporate theme, a prompt to scan a QR code to verify something routine. The employee used their phone. Their corporate inbox scanner never flagged it. By the time IT noticed, credentials were gone. This is not a hypothetical. It is a pattern Microsoft’s threat intelligence teams documented millions of times in the first three months of 2026.

QR code phishing—sometimes called quishing—was the fastest-growing attack vector Microsoft tracked in Q1 2026. The numbers are specific enough to be instructive: attack volumes climbed from 7.6 million in January to 18.7 million in March, a 146% increase over the quarter. That growth didn’t happen gradually. After an initial 35% decline in January, volumes reversed dramatically, rising 59% in February and another 55% in March.

This report sits inside a broader dataset worth understanding on its own terms. On April 30, 2026, Microsoft Threat Intelligence published its Q1 2026 email threat landscape report, drawn from analysis of approximately 8.3 billion email-based phishing threats detected between January and March.

Why QR Codes Became Attackers’ Preferred Weapon

The answer isn’t complicated, but it’s easy to underestimate. Email security gateways are built around one assumption: the threat travels inside the email. They scan text. They follow links. They detonate attachments in sandboxes. They check URLs against reputation feeds. A phishing email containing a PDF attachment with a QR code embedded inside receives none of that scrutiny. The URL sits encoded in a QR image.

There’s also a device-crossing problem that doesn’t get enough attention. An email lands on a managed corporate mailbox, but the QR code invites the user to continue on a personal phone. The company’s email gateway may inspect text and links; the camera app simply sees a code. The user is no longer operating within the corporate security stack—they are crossing devices. Personal phones typically have no enterprise DNS filtering, no conditional access policies, and no Safe Browsing integration tied to corporate credentials.

The PDF Delivery Shift

Delivery method matters here. PDF attachments were the dominant delivery format throughout Q1, growing from 65% of QR code attacks in January to 70% by March. Why PDFs specifically? Because they’re normalized in corporate communications. Invoices, policy updates, onboarding documents, 401K notices—all arrive as PDFs regularly. A PDF prompt asking someone to scan a QR code to confirm account access reads as plausible.

While QR codes embedded directly in email bodies only made up about 5% of attacks, Microsoft noted a 336% increase in that delivery method in March alone. That late-quarter jump suggests attackers are testing what percentage of targets will scan without even opening an attachment. The shorter the friction, the higher the yield.

CAPTCHA Walls: Protecting the Phishing Page, Not the Victim

Alongside QR code growth, Microsoft documented a parallel surge in CAPTCHA-gated phishing. In March, Microsoft tracked 11.9 million CAPTCHA-gated attacks—a 125% surge from earlier in the quarter, and the highest monthly volume in at least a year.

Understanding why this works requires understanding who CAPTCHAs are now built to stop. A real victim solves the CAPTCHA and reaches the fake login page. An automated security crawler—the kind Microsoft, Google, and PhishTank run continuously—gets stuck at the CAPTCHA wall, can’t render the page behind it, and walks away. The phishing URL then stays live on the open web for hours longer than it otherwise would.

This is the inverse of CAPTCHA’s original purpose. What was designed to filter bots away from legitimate users is now being used to filter security scanners away from malicious credential pages.

The Tycoon2FA Disruption—and What It Revealed

Tycoon2FA, the phishing-as-a-service platform operated by the threat actor tracked as Storm-1747, saw its share of CAPTCHA-gated phishing sites fall from over 75% at the end of 2025 to 41% by March 2026—not because the technique stopped working, but because new kits with better cloaking entered the market and split the operator base.

That’s the more important signal. A coordinated takedown effort dented one platform. The technique itself kept spreading to competitors. This broadening of CAPTCHA-gated phishing across an increasing number of threat actors and kits indicates that this technique is becoming a more entrenched component of the phishing playbook rather than a specialty of a small number of tools.

The cybercrime market here operates like any competitive market: a dominant player loses share, competitors absorb the gap, and total volume keeps rising because the underlying product still works.

Credential Theft Is the Dominant Goal—By a Widening Margin

The tactical diversity in delivery methods (PDFs, HTML attachments, SVGs, embedded QR codes) obscures a simpler strategic picture. Credential phishing dominated payload-based attacks throughout Q1, rising from 89% in January to 94% by March. Traditional malware delivery dropped to 5–6% by quarter end.

This reflects where the value is. Stolen credentials are immediately monetizable: they feed business email compromise (BEC) campaigns, enable account takeovers, and get resold to other threat actors. Malware requires more infrastructure, carries more detection risk, and has a longer path to payout.

HTML attachments reached 31% of payloads in March, a 175% increase over the start of the quarter. PDF attachments reached 28% of payloads in March, the highest monthly volume in over a year. Both formats serve the same purpose: move the credential capture form out of the email server’s inspection zone and into a rendering environment the security stack can’t reach before the form appears on screen.

Business Email Compromise: Volume Is Not the Whole Story

Business email compromise remained widespread, with about 10.7 million attacks recorded in the quarter. Much of that activity was driven by generic outreach messages rather than direct payment requests.

That composition detail matters more than the total count. Between 82% and 84% of BEC content was generic outreach—messages designed to elicit a reply rather than steal anything in the initial email. Only 9–10% contained explicit financial or document requests in the first message.

The actual fraud happens in thread two or three, after the target has already replied and established a conversational relationship with what they believe is a trusted contact. The first email’s job is simple: get past the spam filter and earn a response. Security tools that flag “suspicious requests” miss this entirely—because the first suspicious request often doesn’t come until the third email.

The Campaign Scale Makes This Concrete

Individual attack techniques are easier to dismiss as abstract. The campaign data grounds this in operational reality.

Between February 23 and 25, 2026, a single sustained campaign sent more than 1.2 million messages to users at more than 53,000 organizations across 23 countries. Messages used themes including a 401K update, a credit hold warning, a received payment inquiry, and a payment request—each with SVG attachments that, if opened, launched a browser session leading to a CAPTCHA screen and then a fake sign-in page.

A single HTML phishing campaign on March 17 delivered more than 1.5 million confirmed malicious messages to 179,000 organizations across 43 countries.

These are not spray-and-pray operations. The thematic variety within a single campaign—pension updates, credit holds, payment requests—reflects deliberate A/B testing on lure effectiveness, run at scale.

What Security Teams Should Actually Adjust

Three shifts in attacker behavior point to three concrete gaps worth addressing:

QR code scanning in email security. PDF-embedded QR codes bypass link scanning because the URL sits encoded in a QR image. Security teams should review whether their current email security tools can decode and inspect QR codes inside PDF attachments. Many legacy configurations cannot.

Device boundary policies. If employees regularly scan work-related QR codes with personal phones, corporate security policies have a gap that no email gateway can close. Zero-trust access policies need to account for authentication attempts originating from unmanaged devices, even when they follow from managed-device email access.

Authentication methods against AiTM attacks. SMS codes, push prompts, and one-time passwords raised the floor against basic password spraying—they did not end credential theft. Modern adversary-in-the-middle proxy attacks can capture session tokens in real time, bypassing MFA entirely. Microsoft recommends enabling zero-hour auto purge and implementing passwordless authentication through FIDO keys or Microsoft Authenticator. Phishing-resistant FIDO2 keys are the only MFA method that doesn’t pass a usable credential to a proxy sitting between the user and the real service.

The Underlying Pattern

Zoom out from individual techniques and one consistent logic emerges. The headline number is enormous, but the more important story is not volume. Attackers are moving the decisive part of the attack out of the email itself and into infrastructure that can be swapped, screened, redirected, and rebuilt.

The email is increasingly just the first hop. A QR code moves the user to their phone. A PDF bypasses link inspection. A CAPTCHA page blocks the security crawler. An SVG attachment renders the credential form locally. Each technique relocates the critical moment to somewhere the defender isn’t watching.

Security teams are good at thinking in artifacts—hashing files, detonating attachments, stripping macros, blocking known malicious domains. What they are increasingly fighting is not an artifact but a path: click here, scan this, pass this check, land there, surrender credentials.

That shift in architecture is what Q1 2026’s data is actually documenting. The 146% jump in QR code phishing is a symptom. The underlying condition is that the attack chain has been redesigned to evade the specific tools defenders built over the last decade.

Adapting means updating those tools—but it also means accepting that the next delivery format attackers adopt probably isn’t one that appears in this quarter’s report.
