How Big Tech Actually Makes Money

April 30, 2026 11-Minute Read

Most people use Google every day without knowing that Google’s search product has never been the real business. The real business is attention arbitrage: Google captures intent for free and sells the resulting audience to advertisers for billions. That distinction—free product, paid audience—is the structural foundation of at least half of big tech’s revenue. But the models diverge sharply once you get past that surface read, and understanding those divergences matters if you’re investing in, competing with, or building on top of any of these platforms.

Here’s a close look at how each major platform is actually structured to generate revenue—and why the architecture of each model shapes everything from product decisions to regulatory exposure.

Google: Selling Intent at Industrial Scale

Google’s advertising business generated roughly $237 billion in 2024. Of that, the majority came from Search—not YouTube, not Google Maps, not Gmail. Just the text box and ten blue links.

The mechanism is specific: advertisers bid in real-time auctions for placement when a user searches a particular query. Google sets a floor, runs the auction in milliseconds, and charges the winner a price slightly above the second-highest bid (a Vickrey-style auction). The advertiser pays per click, not per impression. This means Google only gets paid when someone acts—a model that aligned Google’s incentives with advertisers for decades.

What makes this unusual isn’t the auction itself. It’s the data flywheel underneath it. Each search query teaches Google something about intent. That data improves ad targeting, which improves click-through rates, which makes the ad product more valuable to advertisers, which funds more infrastructure, which makes search better, which attracts more queries. The loop is self-reinforcing in a way that’s genuinely difficult to replicate.

Why the Search Monopoly Compounds

Google’s 2023 antitrust ruling in the U.S. found the company had illegally maintained its search monopoly, partly through $12–15 billion in annual payments to Apple to remain the default search engine on Safari. That number is instructive: Google was willing to pay the equivalent of a mid-sized country’s GDP annually just to protect default status—suggesting how much default positioning is worth.

The implication for rivals isn’t just about algorithms. It’s about distribution. A better search engine that nobody defaults to is commercially irrelevant.

YouTube and the Shift to Video

YouTube contributes around $35 billion annually to Google’s revenue. It operates on the same ad model as Search but with a critical difference: video inventory is more emotionally engaging, making it competitive with traditional TV advertising budgets. The growth of YouTube Shorts—Google’s response to TikTok—has introduced a new format that monetizes at lower rates per view but at dramatically higher volume.

Meta: Attention Packaged as Demographics

Meta’s business is structurally similar to Google’s—advertising against attention—but the targeting mechanism is completely different. Google targets intent. Meta targets identity.

When you tell Facebook you’re engaged, or when Instagram’s algorithm infers you’re interested in endurance running, that demographic and psychographic data becomes the basis for ad targeting. Advertisers aren’t buying placement against a search query; they’re buying placement in front of a specific human profile. A luxury car brand doesn’t want to show ads to everyone—it wants to show them to 35–55 year-olds with household incomes above $150,000 who recently viewed content related to travel and status goods. Meta can build that audience.

This is why Meta invested so aggressively in cross-site tracking before Apple’s App Tracking Transparency (ATT) policy effectively destroyed it in 2021. ATT required apps to ask users for permission to track them across other apps and websites. Most users declined. Meta estimated the resulting revenue impact at roughly $10 billion in 2022 alone.

The Recovery Was Faster Than Expected

Meta’s response was to rebuild its targeting infrastructure using on-platform signals rather than cross-site data—and to lean harder into AI-driven ad placement that optimizes for outcomes rather than predefined audience segments. By 2023, their ad revenue had recovered and then exceeded pre-ATT levels. The lesson: if your platform generates enough first-party behavioral data, you can weather the loss of third-party signals.

Meta’s bet on WhatsApp business tools and AI-driven customer service products represents the next phase—moving from display advertising toward pay-per-message commercial infrastructure, particularly in markets like India and Brazil where WhatsApp dominates messaging.

Amazon: The Everything Store Was Always a Trojan Horse

Amazon’s e-commerce marketplace operates on thin margins by design. Jeff Bezos described the strategy explicitly in early shareholder letters: Amazon would accept near-zero profitability in retail to build customer loyalty, scale infrastructure, and create competitive moats. That patience paid off, but not primarily in retail.

The real profit engine is Amazon Web Services (AWS), which generated $107 billion in revenue in 2024 and carries operating margins well above 30%—a stark contrast to the low-single-digit margins of retail. AWS didn’t emerge from nowhere; it was built to handle Amazon’s own infrastructure at scale and then offered externally when the team realized the surplus capacity had commercial value. Netflix, Airbnb, and a significant portion of the global internet now run on AWS.

Advertising: The Quietly Massive Third Business

Amazon’s advertising segment—largely sponsored product placements within search results on Amazon.com—exceeded $56 billion in 2024. This tends to surprise people, because Amazon isn’t typically framed as an advertising company. But the logic is tight: when someone searches “running shoes” on Amazon, they are in an extremely high purchase-intent moment. That’s more commercially valuable than almost any other context for an ad impression.

The result is that brands now treat Amazon advertising as a necessity, not an option. Your product can be on Amazon and still be effectively invisible unless you’re bidding for sponsored placement.

The Third-Party Seller Trap

Roughly 60% of units sold on Amazon come from third-party sellers, not Amazon itself. Those sellers pay referral fees (typically 8–15% of the sale price), fulfillment fees if they use Fulfillment by Amazon (FBA), advertising fees to stay visible, and subscription fees for seller accounts. Amazon has essentially built a toll road—and made the road so valuable that sellers have little choice but to pay the tolls.

Apple: Hardware as a Platform

Apple’s revenue model is the outlier in this group. Unlike Google or Meta, Apple doesn’t sell its users’ attention. It sells them products—expensive ones, with intentional upgrade friction built into the ecosystem.

The iPhone generates roughly half of Apple’s total revenue. But the strategic value of the iPhone isn’t the hardware margin alone (though that’s substantial—estimated gross margins of 35–40% on premium models). The iPhone is the distribution layer for Apple’s services business, which includes the App Store, Apple TV+, Apple Music, iCloud, and Apple Pay.

The App Store as a Toll Booth

Apple takes a 15–30% cut of every in-app purchase and subscription sold through App Store on iOS. On a platform of 1.4 billion active devices, that commission is structurally enormous—and has been the source of antitrust battles with Epic Games, Spotify, and regulators in the EU and Japan.

The EU’s Digital Markets Act forced Apple to allow alternative app stores and third-party browser engines on iOS in Europe starting in 2024. Apple complied, technically, while building in conditions that critics called “malicious compliance”—including a per-install “Core Technology Fee” that some developers argued would cost them more than the commission structure they replaced. The legal and regulatory story isn’t settled.

Services Margin vs. Hardware Volume

Apple’s Services segment carries gross margins above 70%. Hardware margins are meaningful but lower. This is why Apple has methodically expanded its services offerings over the past decade—it’s not that iPhone hardware isn’t profitable, it’s that software-attached revenue is structurally more profitable at scale. A subscription renews without requiring Apple to manufacture, ship, or support additional physical product.

Microsoft: The Enterprise Conversion

Microsoft’s business model is probably the least dramatic story in this group, which is part of why it tends to get underestimated.

The core of Microsoft’s revenue is cloud infrastructure (Azure) and enterprise software (Office 365, Dynamics, LinkedIn). Azure competes directly with AWS and Google Cloud in a market where enterprise switching costs are high and long-term contracts are the norm. Microsoft’s advantage is the installed base: most enterprises already use Windows, Active Directory, and Office. Azure becomes a natural extension rather than a new vendor relationship.

The OpenAI Bet

Microsoft’s $13 billion investment in OpenAI (across multiple rounds) and its rapid integration of AI features into every major product—Word, Excel, Teams, Azure, Bing—is not primarily a bet on search. It’s a bet on enterprise software lock-in. Copilot features embedded in Office 365 make the product harder to replace, increase per-seat pricing, and give Microsoft a new usage-based revenue lever.

The irony is that Microsoft’s OpenAI investment revived Bing, which had been a sustained failure for two decades. Bing’s market share jumped from roughly 3% to 5–6% in 2023 after the AI integration launch—modest in absolute terms, but meaningful in a market where a percentage point represents billions in annual ad revenue.

Netflix and Spotify: Subscription as the Alternative Architecture

For contrast: Netflix and Spotify operate on subscription models that actively avoid the ad-attention loop—or did, until they didn’t.

Netflix’s original model was simple: flat monthly fee, unlimited content. No ads, no algorithms surfacing sponsored content, no demographic targeting. This made the product clean and the relationship with users relatively uncomplicated. The problem was content cost inflation. Netflix spent $17 billion on content in 2023. That spending requires subscriber growth to justify, and subscriber growth has a ceiling.

Netflix’s ad-supported tier, launched in 2022, was a structural concession to that math. Lower subscription price, ad revenue from brands, same content. By 2024, the ad-supported tier represented a significant and growing portion of new subscriber additions.

Spotify followed a similar path in podcasting—acquiring exclusive shows, then abandoning exclusivity when the strategy underperformed, then launching its own ad tech platform. The company has never turned a consistent annual profit despite reaching 600+ million users. The record label licensing structure, which requires Spotify to pay out the majority of streaming revenue to rights holders, creates a margin ceiling that the company has been engineering around with audiobooks, podcasts, and its own direct artist deals.

The One Pattern That Unifies All of Them

Every model above, regardless of surface-level differences, depends on some version of the same insight: scale changes the economics of software.

The marginal cost of serving one more search result, one more ad impression, one more stream, or one more API call approaches zero as infrastructure costs are amortized across billions of users. This is why tech platforms can appear cheap or free—the cost structure allows it. And it’s why the early race for scale in each of these markets was existential rather than incremental. Winning at scale means you can sustain a cost structure that nobody smaller can match.

The business model question for any new platform, then, isn’t just “how do we make money?” It’s “what do we need to scale to before the unit economics turn positive?” Google spent years subsidizing search. Amazon subsidized retail. Spotify is still in that phase, arguably.

What This Means for Builders and Investors

A few structural observations worth sitting with:

  • Platform dependence is expensive. Whether you’re an app developer on iOS, a seller on Amazon, or a brand advertiser on Meta, you are paying rent on someone else’s infrastructure. The more essential the platform, the higher the rent over time.

  • First-party data is becoming the moat. As privacy regulations and browser/OS changes restrict third-party tracking, platforms that own the relationship with users—Apple, Amazon, TikTok—have structural advantages over those that historically relied on cross-site data.

  • AI integration is a pricing lever, not just a feature. Microsoft’s Copilot pricing (an additional $30/user/month on top of Microsoft 365) showed that AI features can justify meaningful per-unit price increases in enterprise software. Google’s Gemini integration into Workspace follows the same logic. Expect this pattern to define enterprise software pricing for the next five years.

The business models of major tech platforms aren’t static. They mutate in response to regulatory pressure, competitor moves, and shifts in where user attention migrates. What holds constant is the underlying logic: find something people do at scale, insert yourself into the infrastructure of that activity, and capture a percentage of the value created.

Understanding that logic is more useful than memorizing current revenue figures—because the figures change, and the logic doesn’t.
