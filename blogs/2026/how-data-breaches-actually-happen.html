How Data Breaches Actually Happen

April 29, 2026 10-Minute Read

Most people picture a data breach as a hooded figure hammering away at a keyboard while lines of code scroll past. The reality is considerably less cinematic—and more unsettling. The majority of breaches don’t involve sophisticated zero-day exploits or nation-state hackers outmaneuvering elite security teams. They involve a sales rep clicking the wrong link on a Tuesday afternoon, or a cloud storage bucket left open because no one thought to check the default settings.

Understanding how data breaches actually happen matters because the threat model most people carry around is wrong. And a wrong threat model leads to bad decisions.

The Entry Point Is Almost Never Where You’d Expect

Phishing: Not the Dumb Attack It’s Made Out to Be

Phishing gets dismissed as an “obvious” threat, which is exactly why it keeps working. Modern phishing campaigns don’t arrive as misspelled emails from Nigerian princes. They arrive as a Docusign notification for a contract you were expecting, or a Slack message from someone whose display name looks exactly like your IT administrator’s.

In the 2023 MGM Resorts breach, attackers reportedly used LinkedIn to identify an employee, then called the IT help desk impersonating that person. No malware. No code. Just a phone call and some social engineering. Within minutes, they had enough access to eventually cause an estimated $100 million in damages.

The mechanism isn’t technical—it’s cognitive. People are conditioned to respond to urgency, authority, and familiarity. A well-crafted phishing email exploits all three simultaneously.

Credential Stuffing: The Boring Attack That Never Stops Working

When a breach happens at one company, those usernames and passwords get sold, traded, and eventually dumped publicly. Attackers then run automated tools that try those credentials across hundreds of other services. This is credential stuffing—and it works at a frightening scale because password reuse is still endemic.

A 2024 analysis of a major credential leak found that roughly 65% of exposed passwords had been seen in a previous breach. People change their passwords when forced to, usually by appending “1” or “!” to the old one.

The implication: a breach at a small e-commerce site you signed up for six years ago can become the entry point for your email, your bank, or your employer’s VPN.

What Happens After Someone Gets In

The Dwell Time Problem

Here’s a number that changes how you think about breaches: the average time between an attacker gaining access and an organization detecting that access has historically ranged from weeks to months. IBM’s data puts the average at around 194 days to identify a breach.

That’s not 194 days of an alarm going off while the security team tries to respond. That’s 194 days of an attacker quietly moving through systems, escalating privileges, mapping the network, and identifying what’s worth taking—while everyone else goes about their normal workday.

This dwell time is why “we got hacked” stories often end up being “we got hacked six months ago and just found out.” The breach event and the discovery event are rarely the same moment.

Lateral Movement: From Foothold to Full Compromise

Getting one employee’s credentials is a start. Getting the entire customer database requires moving laterally—hopping from one system to another using the access you have to acquire access you shouldn’t have.

In practice, this looks like: compromise an employee’s laptop, find saved credentials for an internal application, use those to access a server, discover that server has overly permissive access to a database, extract the database.

Each step seems minor in isolation. Together they form a path from a phishing email to 10 million customer records.

The Structural Vulnerabilities That Make It Possible

Third-Party Access: The Weakest Link That Rarely Gets Audited

The 2013 Target breach—still studied in security circles—didn’t start with Target. It started with a HVAC contractor. Attackers compromised a vendor that had remote access to Target’s network for billing and contract purposes. From there, they pivoted to payment systems and eventually captured credit card data from 40 million customers.

Most large organizations have dozens, sometimes hundreds, of third-party vendors with some level of network access. The security rigor applied to those vendors is usually far weaker than what’s applied internally.

This is the supply chain problem. Your security is only as strong as the least-secured entity with access to your systems.

Misconfigured Cloud Storage

Between 2017 and 2022, a remarkable number of high-profile breaches came down to a single, embarrassingly simple issue: someone had set an Amazon S3 bucket to “public” when it should have been private.

The data wasn’t stolen in the traditional sense—it was just sitting there, accessible to anyone who looked for it. In several cases, the buckets were discovered not by the companies that owned them but by security researchers running automated scans.

The failure isn’t technical complexity. It’s process. No one reviewed the configuration. No one asked “who can access this?” as a matter of routine.

Unpatched Software: The Attack Surface That Compounds Over Time

The 2017 Equifax breach exposed the personal data of 147 million Americans. The vulnerability that made it possible—a flaw in the Apache Struts framework—had a patch available for two months before attackers exploited it. Equifax hadn’t applied it.

This is the unglamorous reality of most infrastructure breaches: attackers are often exploiting vulnerabilities that defenders already know about and have a fix for. The gap is execution, not information.

Unpatched systems accumulate over time. One server that “can’t be taken down for maintenance” becomes two, then ten. Each one is a potential entry point with a known map that attackers can follow.

Insider Threats: The Category No One Wants to Talk About

Not every breach involves an external attacker. A meaningful percentage of incidents are caused or enabled by people already inside the organization—either intentionally or through negligence.

Intentional insider threats are relatively rare but disproportionately damaging. A database administrator with access to everything and a reason to act—a pending layoff, a financial dispute, an ideological grievance—can exfiltrate data in ways that are difficult to distinguish from normal activity.

Negligence is far more common. The employee who emails a spreadsheet of customer data to their personal email “to work on it over the weekend.” The contractor who stores sensitive credentials in a shared Notion document. The developer who commits an API key to a public GitHub repository.

The 2022 LastPass breach, which ultimately exposed encrypted customer password vaults, was significantly worsened by an engineer having access to production systems from a personal laptop with outdated software. One endpoint, insufficient controls, catastrophic downstream consequences.

Why Encryption Isn’t the Full Story

A common assumption: if data is encrypted, a breach doesn’t matter much. This is partially true and largely misleading.

Encryption protects data at rest—if someone steals a hard drive, the contents are unreadable without the key. But most breaches extract data while it’s being processed, not while it’s sitting on disk. At the point of use, data has to be decrypted. Attackers who have access to a running system often have access to data in its decrypted state.

Additionally, encryption doesn’t protect against breaches that target credentials rather than data directly. If someone logs into your system with legitimate credentials, they see what a legitimate user sees—encryption doesn’t factor in at all.

The Human Element Isn’t a Bug, It’s the Target

Security teams have spent decades trying to “fix” human behavior through training programs, phishing simulations, and acceptable-use policies. These measures help at the margins, but they rest on a flawed premise: that with enough education, people will stop making mistakes.

Attackers don’t need everyone to make mistakes. They need one person, once, under the right conditions.

A fatigued employee at 5pm on a Friday who receives what looks like an urgent email from their CEO is not a failure of training. It’s a predictable outcome of being human. Security systems that depend on zero human errors will fail because they’re designed around a fictional user.

The more durable approach—implemented by organizations that consistently handle breaches better—is layered defense that assumes mistakes will happen and contains the damage when they do. Multi-factor authentication, network segmentation, and least-privilege access don’t prevent breaches. They make breaches significantly harder to complete and significantly easier to detect.

What “A Breach” Actually Looks Like, End to End

To make this concrete: here’s a simplified but realistic attack chain.

  1. Attacker identifies a target company and finds an employee on LinkedIn with a relevant role.
  2. They craft a spearphishing email referencing a real project (scraped from public sources) and include a malicious document.
  3. Employee opens the document. A macro runs. A remote access tool installs silently.
  4. Attacker uses that access over several weeks to map the internal network during off-hours.
  5. They find credentials for a database server stored in a configuration file on the employee’s machine.
  6. Using those credentials, they access the database server and run queries to identify high-value tables.
  7. They compress and exfiltrate the data in small chunks over several weeks to avoid triggering volume alerts.
  8. The company discovers the breach 180 days later, after a third party alerts them to data for sale on a dark web forum.

At no point in that chain did the attacker need to write a novel exploit or breach a military-grade firewall. They needed patience, a convincing email, and a few configurations that nobody had reviewed lately.

The Gap Between Security Spending and Security Outcomes

Global cybersecurity spending has grown steadily for years—projected to exceed $200 billion annually by 2026. Breach frequency has not decreased proportionally. This isn’t evidence that security tools don’t work. It’s evidence that spending on tools without investing equally in process, visibility, and response creates a false sense of protection.

Companies buy endpoint detection software but don’t staff anyone to review its alerts. They implement multi-factor authentication—but only for external-facing applications, leaving internal systems untouched. They run annual penetration tests but don’t remediate the findings quickly enough for it to matter.

Security theater—the performance of security without the substance of it—is widespread. And it’s visible to attackers in ways it isn’t visible to the executives approving the security budget.

Where This Leaves Us

Data breaches are not primarily a technology problem with a technology solution. They’re the result of attackers being patient, adaptive, and incentivized—while defenders are dealing with competing priorities, limited budgets, and the operational complexity of real organizations.

The entry points are known. Phishing. Credential reuse. Unpatched systems. Third-party access. Misconfiguration. These aren’t novel attack vectors—they’ve been the top causes of breaches for years running. What changes is the execution, the scale, and the targets.

Understanding this should shift the question from “how do we prevent breaches entirely” to “how do we make breaches harder to execute, faster to detect, and less catastrophic when they happen.” That framing is less satisfying but considerably more honest—and it tends to produce better security decisions.
